The Story of Sindhi Hindus

Ancient Period

2600 BCE - 900 CE

Hinduism has its roots in the Indus Valley Civilization that developed in what is now Pakistan and northwestern India around 2600 BCE to 1900 BCE. Hindu communities have been present in Sindh for thousands of years, with the region being ruled by Hindu kingdoms and empires such as the Sindhudesh Kingdom and the Rajput-era Kingdom of Alor. During this time, Hinduism continued to evolve and diversify, with the development of new philosophical schools of thought and the composition of sacred texts such as the Vedas and the Upanishads.

Medieval Period

700 CE - 1600 CE

During the 7th century, Arab armies invaded and conquered Sindh, bringing Islam to the region. During the Mughal period (1526-1857), Hindus faced religious persecution and discrimination, but continued to maintain a significant presence in Sindh. During this time, Hinduism also saw the development of Bhakti movements, which emphasized devotion and personal connection to a deity as a means of spiritual liberation.

British Rule

1843 CE - 1947 CE

In 1843, the British East India Company took control of Sindh, leading to the establishment of British colonial rule in the region. During this period, Hindu communities in Sindh faced further discrimination and marginalization, but were able to maintain their religious and cultural traditions through the establishment of religious institutions and the preservation of sacred texts. The Hindu reform movements of the 19th and early 20th centuries, such as the Brahmo Samaj and the Arya Samaj, also gained traction in Sindh and sought to modernize and rejuvenate Hinduism.

Post-Partition

1947 CE - Present

In 1947, with the partition of India, Hindu communities in Sindh were forced to flee to India as refugees due to the violence and chaos that followed independence. Today, there is a small Hindu community in Sindh, but their numbers and influence have diminished significantly since the partition. Nevertheless, Hindu religious and cultural traditions continue to play an important role in the region's history and heritage, and Hindu pilgrims still visit sites such as the Shiva temple in Hala, the Jain temples in Hyderabad, and the Sufi shrines of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar and Sachal Sarmast.

"Sindhi" (Khudabadi Script)

The Sindhi script is one of rich history and cultural significance. Initially, the script was written in the Khudabadi script, a script derived from the Brahmi script used in ancient India. However, after the Arab conquest of Sindh, the script was gradually replaced by the Arabic script. As a result, many Sindhi Hindus today do not know how to write in the original Sindhi script, and it is primarily only spoken among them. The loss of this script is a significant loss to the Sindhi culture and heritage. Despite the efforts to revive the script, it remains an endangered yet important symbol of Sindhi identity. Many organizations and individuals continue to work to preserve and promote the use of Sindhi script, both in Sindh and abroad.
